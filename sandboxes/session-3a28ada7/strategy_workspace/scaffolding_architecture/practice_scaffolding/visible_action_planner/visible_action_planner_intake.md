# INTAKE — Visible-Action Planner

## Summary
Daily planning ritual: what to do, when, for how long. Bridges knowing-doing gap. Uses implementation intentions (if-then planning) and shifts work to morning/early afternoon. Designed to separate "action" from "identity verdict" — Maxim acts before deciding what the action means about him.

## Question this route answers
"What is the concrete implementation of the Visible-Action Planner — how do we structure daily planning, what if-then rules work for Maxim's psychology, and how do we make the planner obsolete as Maxim internalizes initiation?"

Position in family: Depth 4, sub-component 2 of 3 under Practice Scaffolding. Sibling routes: Journaling Protocol, Submission Rhythm Tracker.

## Assumptions
- Maxim's primary bottleneck is initiation, not ability (memory-confirmed).
- A visible, concrete plan reduces the emotional activation cost of every session (addresses "every session requires fresh emotional activation").
- Implementation intentions (if-then) work for Maxim's systematic cognitive style — he will appreciate the structured approach.
- Maxim will resist if the planner feels like "another system to maintain" — must be minimal (1 decision per day: "What is my ONE action?").
- The planner must be visible (sticky note, smartwatch face, or desktop wallpaper) — not buried in an app.
- "Action before identity verdict" framing: Maxim writes what he will do, NOT what it means about him.
- Morning planning (first 5 minutes of day) is the optimal time — sets intention before depletion.

## Dependencies and permissions
- Visible planning medium (sticky note on monitor, smartwatch complication, or pinned note).
- Permission to send 1 morning prompt: "What is your ONE action today?"
- Permission to send 1 afternoon check-in: "Did you do the ONE action? If not, what's the smallest step forward?"
- 2-5 minutes for morning planning, 1 minute for check-in.
- Maxim's explicit understanding that this is scaffolding — prompts decrease as self-initiation increases.
- If-then rule is non-negotiable: "At [time], in [location], I will do [specific action]" — but the action is Maxim's choice.

## What this route enables
- **Initiation without identity verdict:** Maxim acts without deciding what the action means about him.
- **Early-day optimization:** Shifts work to morning/early afternoon, bypassing the evening depletion trap.
- **Visible proof:** A completed action checklist provides concrete evidence of change.
- **Decision fatigue reduction:** One decision per day ("what is my ONE action?") replaces the overwhelming question "what should I do?"
- **If-then automation:** "When X happens, I do Y" reduces need for willpower.
- **Obsolescence by internalization:** The planner fades as Maxim naturally starts asking "what's my one action today?" without prompting.
- **School-task targeting:** The ONE action can always be a school submission (bridges to Submission Rhythm Tracker).

## What could break it
- **System resistance:** "I don't need a planner" — Maxim may reject structured planning as infantilizing.
- **Perfectionism:** If Maxim sets too-ambitious ONE actions and fails, the planner becomes a shame source.
- **Over-planning:** Maxim may expand the planner into a full productivity system (intellectualization loop).
- **Skipping mornings:** If morning planning is missed, the day has no anchor — may fall back to reactive mode.
- **Tool dependency:** Maxim cannot start any action without the planner (dependency trap).
- **Summer schedule disruption:** Different summer schedule means different planning needs — May-June rhythm may not survive July.

## Main unknowns
1. Maxim's optimal productive window (memory suggests morning/early afternoon but needs confirmation).
2. Whether Maxim has tried daily planning before and what happened.
3. Maxim's attitude toward structured planning ("that's for people who can't handle chaos" vs. "useful tool").
4. Whether the "ONE action" framing is enough or if Maxim needs more granular planning.

## Evidence that matters most
- **Memory (initiation bottleneck):** "The remaining bottleneck is participation, oral answers, practice, emotional buy-in, and consistency." The Visible-Action Planner directly addresses this.
- **Memory (identity threat):** School tasks feel like identity verdicts. The planner separates action from meaning: "Write down what you'll do, not what it says about you."
- **Memory (evening depletion):** "After around 20:00 it gets hard to work." Morning planning + early-day execution bypasses this.
- **Web (implementation intentions):** Gollwitzer (2008) — if-then planning significantly increases initiation rates. Holds for ADHD brains.

- **Web (executive function basics):** CHADD, Child Mind Institute — task breakdown, time estimation, weekly planning are front-line evidence-based interventions for executive function challenges.
- **Web (One-Tracker Method):** "Your teen opens one screen that shows today's assignments." The "ONE action" is the same principle — minimal cognitive load.
- **Parent route intake (early-day optimization):** "Practice scaffolding shifts Maxim's work window to morning/early afternoon."
